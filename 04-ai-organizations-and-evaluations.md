Some of the other things that we need to think about when we talk about an AI-based company is: we have new ways and new workflows and new processes in terms of how we achieve outcomes or work gets done.

**Agents don't have the same challenges as human beings** — not that they don't have challenges and constraints, but they're quite different. The problem of hallucination, jagged intelligence, a lot of domain-specific expertise gaps, challenges in terms of memory and context, and even things like survival instinct. But at the same time, they don't need a break. They can iteratively and persistently work towards a particular objective without getting tired. They can reference from a large scope of work actively. They can intelligently plan and reason about how to solve a particular problem with the available tools. And of course, the breadth of those tools determines how large a space they can operate in — that itself is a limiting factor today, but as we keep expanding, we're trying to figure out where we can put AIs throughout the organization.

It can be in the form of robotics in factories, delivery agents, self-driving cars and vehicles and trucks. Large parts of your workforce can now become really predictable, really well-trained, and operate within a certain workflow or standard operating procedure. **Managers of the future will have to create that and set that up** to ensure that your AI is able to effectively deliver within the outcomes the organization is seeking.

This definitely points to the fact that there is going to be a lot of human involvement in this process where we figure out: how, in the interest of shareholders and an important stakeholder — our customers and consumers — can you actually develop AI processes that are able to deliver and create value? You'd want to think about reviews, following procedures, tests, running simulations, having humans in the loop, doing audits, having a system of record. Allow the AI to unblock a lot of the friction which traditional humans would have had inside the process. I don't wait for my AI agent to create documents — I get it to present it to me in the end, make a PPT. So you can just skip through a lot of inherent friction points.

We have these in organizations because running a large organization requires a lot of coordination of resources — economic resources: labor, capital flowing through, land, assets flowing through, interacting. Labor, capital and technology interacting.

So in this new paradigm, an important aspect is: **how do you know if your AI is really doing good?** The approach which the industry is broadly in alignment on today is **evaluations**. One of the second most popular skills which everyone says product managers need to learn: you need to know how to write prompts and you need to know how to run evals. Two fundamentally very important pillars.
