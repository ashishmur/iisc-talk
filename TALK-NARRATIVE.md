# AI & The New Management Playbook
## IISc DoMS Guest Lecture — Friday, March 6, 2026

**Duration:** 90 min (60-65 min talk + 25-30 min Q&A)
**Audience:** Cross-departmental — M.Mgt, PhD, engineering, science students
**Emotional arc:** Tension → Context → Understanding → Proof → Relief

---

## ACT I: THE TENSION (10 min)

### 1. Opening — The Anxiety Poll (2 min)

Start with a show of hands:

> "How many of you feel anxious about your future — your career, your relevance — because of AI?"

Most hands will go up. Acknowledge it. Don't dismiss it.

> "Good. That feeling is real. And it's not new. Hold that thought — we'll come back to it at the end."

This sets up the tension that the entire talk resolves.

### 2. The VUCA World (8 min)

Introduce the VUCA framework — Volatility, Uncertainty, Complexity, Ambiguity. We keep saying we live in an "increasingly VUCA world" as though it's cyclical. It isn't. It's a one-way street going upwards.

But here's the perspective shift: **this is part of a larger pattern.** The world is almost always starting afresh and then building up — and in the build-up, you see these flavors of VUCA emerge. Then a technological disruption resets the cycle.

Trace the pattern using Carlota Perez's framework of technological surges (from *Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital*, 2002). Each surge lasts 45-60 years and follows four phases: Irruption, Frenzy, Synergy, Maturity.

A cleaner abstraction for the audience:
- **Physical Power** (steam engines, mechanization, 1770s onwards) → replaced muscle
- **Electrical Power** (grid, factories, mass production, 1870s onwards) → amplified energy
- **Information Power** (computers, internet, software, 1970s onwards) → organized knowledge
- **Cognitive Power** (AI, agents, LLMs, 2020s onwards) → replaces thought itself

Each wave augmented what came before. But this one is different: for the first time, we are augmenting the thing that did the augmenting. This is Schumpeter's "creative destruction" operating on the cognitive layer.

Technology has always occupied a central place in the fundamental economic equation: **land, labor, capital, and technology.** The largest wealth-creating firms in the world today are all technology companies. All the billionaires come from there.

But here's where the anxiety comes from: every previous revolution replaced physical or operational labor. People could always move "up" to more cognitive work. AI is the first technology that targets the cognitive layer directly. When technology replaces the mind, not just the muscle, the escape route that worked for 250 years may no longer exist.

We are currently at the outset of technology **fundamentally replacing labor** in this equation. And this time, it's not just factory labor. It's the labor of thinking.

## ACT II: THE PATTERN (12 min)

### 3. Technology vs. Labor — A History (5 min)

Every technology cycle has followed the same script:
1. Technology arrives → anxiety about jobs
2. Physical labor gets displaced → people move "up" to operational roles
3. Operational roles get displaced → people move "up" to intellectual roles
4. Each time, new job categories are created that didn't exist before

The software programmer never existed until it did. It has been a driving part of our labor and economy story for decades.

But there's a pattern within the pattern: **we've been steadily climbing a ladder, and we may be running out of rungs.**

- Technology removed the need for physical labor
- Technology removed the need for routine operational labor
- Technology is now removing the need for intellectual labor

> "If you don't need intellectual labor anymore, what's left? You sell your time, or you sell your capital. And at that point, only capital owners have a role left in society. Labor has nowhere else to move. We've hit the ceiling."

Pause. Let that land.

> "Will new jobs still be created? Maybe. But the honest answer is: beyond intellect, what is it that we will be able to contribute? I don't believe we have an answer for that yet."

### 4. The Counter-Narrative: Robots & the Physical Frontier (3 min)

Elon Musk talks about the "golden era of hardware jobs." The robotics trend:
- Tesla's Optimus
- Unitree
- Hyundai's robotics division

When you can embody AI — give it a body to think, look at the world, and operate in the world — you're talking about the complete replacement of human labor across every dimension. Not just information work. Everything.

### 5. So Where Are We Today? (4 min)

Introduce **OpenAI's five levels of AGI** as a map:

| Level | Capability | Status |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| 1 | Conversational AI | ChatGPT — done |
| 2 | Reasoning | Chain-of-thought, problem-solving — done |
| 3 | Agents | Systems that plan, reason, act, and learn — **we are here** |
| 4 | AI Organizations | Multiple agents coordinating — emerging |
| 5 | AGI | General intelligence across all domains — unknown |

> "We are at Level 3. Agents. And that's already changed everything."

Define agents clearly:

> "An agent is a system that can pursue a goal repeatedly — by reasoning, by planning, by using tools to act inside an environment, and having memory and context because of which it's continuously learning."

For the first time, the entire **signal-to-closure loop** — where a signal triggers a workflow, a worker understands the situation, builds cognition, finds an answer, takes action, and closes the loop — has been completely automated.

## ACT III: GROUND ZERO — SOFTWARE (15 min)

### 6. Coding: The First Industry to Fall (5 min)

Why software first?
- 100% of the work exists as data (code files, documentation, git history)
- The signal-to-closure loop is fully digital — no physical component
- Rich training data available worldwide

The result: **single engineers now spin up 20 agents that read a spec document and build the entire product end-to-end** — write code, test it, deploy it, even record a video walkthrough for review.

Key tweets to reference:
- **Andrej Karpathy** (ex-Tesla AI, ex-OpenAI): coined "vibe coding" — top developers haven't written code by hand in 100+ days
- **Naval Ravikant**: "Product management is vibe coding. Engineering is prompt engineering."

> "The cost to build software has come down to zero. That's not hyperbole. That's what's happening right now."

Implications:
- Even playing field — no domain expertise barrier, no expensive labor to acquire
- **Deflation** inside the software economy
- You don't have to be literate in engineering to build a product. Anyone can.

### 7. What Happens When Production Costs Hit Zero (5 min)

If anyone can build software, what matters?

**Distribution and marketing.** A friend who runs a tech consulting firm told me: "The new job for everyone has become marketing." When production is free, the battle shifts to: who can reach customers?

**Blue Ocean / Red Ocean Strategy** (Kim & Mauborgne, 2005): A red ocean is an existing market with defined boundaries and accepted rules, where companies fight over shrinking demand. A blue ocean is an uncontested market space where demand is created. The key insight for AI: **AI doesn't just make red oceans bloodier. It turns every blue ocean red before you can finish swimming.** When the cost of building drops to zero, barriers to entry collapse, and every market floods with competitors almost instantly. Products commoditize at unprecedented speed.

**Porter's Five Forces** all intensify simultaneously when AI enters:
- Threat of new entrants: dramatically increases (anyone can build)
- Bargaining power of buyers: increases (AI-powered comparison, near-zero switching costs)
- Threat of substitutes: explodes (AI creates substitutes from adjacent industries)
- Competitive rivalry: intensifies (faster innovation cycles, rapid feature cloning)
- Supplier (labor) power: inverts (AI can substitute for many tasks)

> "Porter's Five Forces assumed friction. AI removes friction. When every force intensifies simultaneously, the only sustainable advantage is the speed at which you learn and adapt."

Real consequences already playing out:
- **Block** announced 50% layoffs specifically due to AI
- IT company stocks down 25-30%, markets pricing in the death of the old model
- American companies recorded **1.2 million layoffs in 2025, up 58% from the year prior** (Acemoglu), with more than 50,000 directly tied to AI
- Every company in tech is going through the **innovator's dilemma**, but unlike Kodak, most are pivoting aggressively because they see the wall clearly

> "The frequency with which AI is mentioned in financial disclosures has exploded. Companies without an AI strategy are being punished by markets."

### 8. The Economics: Deflation vs. Abundance (5 min)

Two paths are playing out simultaneously, and the honest answer is we don't know which one wins:

**Path A: The Abundance Thesis** (Sam Altman, David Autor)
- Hundreds of people in a process loop = cost, time, inefficiency, failure
- Remove them with AI → fundamental deflation → goods become cheaper → era of abundance
- Sam Altman envisions everything (housing, education, food) becoming **half as expensive every two years**
- David Autor (MIT) argues AI can **democratize expertise**: letting nurses do some of what doctors do, paralegals do what lawyers do. This could rebuild the middle class.
- The worker who is 3x more productive serves 3x more clients, converts 3x more leads. The company grows.

**Path B: The Intensification Thesis** (Daron Acemoglu, HBR 2026 study)
- HBR studied 200 employees for 8 months: **AI did not reduce work. It intensified it.** Workers reported 12-hour days. 62% reported burnout.
- When a worker finishes 3x faster, the work isn't elastic. The company realizes it needs 1/3 the workforce.
- Goldman Sachs (March 2026): found "basically zero" relationship between AI adoption and productivity at the economy-wide level
- 95% of CEOs expect to conduct layoffs in the next five years because of AI
- Acemoglu: "If we go down this path of destroying jobs and creating more inequality, U.S. democracy is not going to survive."

> "The question is not whether AI makes you more productive. The question is: who captures that productivity? You, or your employer's balance sheet?"

**The honest framing:** We don't know which path wins. But the policy choices being made right now, by companies and governments, will determine which world we end up in. Every previous technology revolution replaced muscle. This one replaces mind. **Anyone who tells you they know exactly how this ends is selling something.**

**Where does value accrue in the AI stack?**

Draw the value chain:
```
Energy → Chips → Fabricators → Frontier Labs → Hyperscalers → Application Layer → Distribution
```

Key insight: **the model is a commodity.** Anthropic refused a U.S. government contract — the government just moved to the next provider. At Microsoft, we have access to every model. No preferences. Everything is interchangeable. The value isn't in the model. It's in:
- **Compute** (the infrastructure layer)
- **Context** (organizational knowledge, workflows, processes)
- **Distribution** (reaching customers)

Another insight: **agents don't need UI.** UI was built for humans. Agents communicate through protocols like Anthropic's MCP (Model Context Protocol). The application layer — all those apps, websites, products — is under threat. Your AI just talks to other AIs and gets things done. Why would you subject yourself to UI friction?

## ACT IV: FROM THE INSIDE (10 min)

### 9. Microsoft's AI Journey — What We Learned (5 min)

Personal credibility moment. I drive the development of the Excel agent at Microsoft — one of the flagship AI-native product experiences. Here's what we learned about deploying AI internally:

**The top-down approach failed.** Telling people "here's how to use AI to be productive" didn't work. Too much nuance in individual information work.

**What worked: bottom-up adoption.**
- Put unlimited compute in the hands of product managers
- Push people to dedicate time to figuring out what they can do with it
- Tie incentive structures to AI usage
- Philosophy: the more you use it and understand the patterns, the better you figure out what to build for customers

Software engineers who've adopted this are seeing **20x productivity** — and that's at Microsoft, with our own tools.

### 10. Project Nebula — Building the Muscle (5 min)

This is the program I conceived and lead. A mandatory, in-person weekly AI upskilling program for product managers across Microsoft India.

The setup: 100 PMs, 600 engineers, 40-50 designers. All of us are:
- Learning to generate better ideas with AI
- Creating mockups and visual prototypes
- Building working prototypes with models inside them
- Deploying AI workflows into everyday work
- Turning discoveries into learning modules for the org

> "When new people join, they don't start from scratch. They inherit the tribal knowledge we've accumulated — how we've unlocked opportunity and productivity using AI — and they ramp up from there."

**The learning for all of you:** For the next 1-2 years, there is a massive wave where organizations across industries will start adopting AI. **The most valuable managers will be the ones who figure out how to diffuse that technology throughout the company.** That's the skill set organizations are hiring for right now.

## ACT V: THE NEW PLAYBOOK (15 min)

### 11. AI Organizations — Designing for Agents (5 min)

Agents have different constraints than humans:
- **Weaknesses:** hallucination, jagged intelligence, limited domain expertise, memory/context challenges
- **Strengths:** tireless, persistent, can reference massive scope, can plan and reason with available tools, operate 24/7

> "Managers of the future will design workflows and SOPs for AI agents the way managers of the past designed them for human teams."

Here's the key insight: **the best way to design AI systems is to replicate organizational structures that companies have spent a century optimizing.**

Walk through the PM workflow as an example:
1. Strategic paper → 2. User research → 3. PRD → 4. Design → 5. Technical design → 6. Jira tickets → 7. Individual engineers execute → 8. Git for coordination → 9. Unit tests for quality

Now replace each step with an agent. Break the work into chunks. Each agent has limited context — just like each employee has a limited role. The coordination patterns are the same: Git, code review, testing.

> "Your ability to understand the process, replace labor with compute, and say 'I understand how compute can create value in the form of a workflow' — that's the new core skill."

**Two skills every manager needs now:**
1. **Prompting** — the ability to instruct AI precisely
2. **Evaluations** — the ability to measure if AI is doing a good job (think: unit tests for intelligence)

### 12. The Age of the Polymath (5 min)

What's happening at Microsoft right now:
- I need to be a good designer — I'm expected to bring in polished visual prototypes
- I need to write working code — I can't say "that's engineering's job" anymore
- Leadership says: **if you can't prototype it, that idea can't be built**

Everyone is becoming a generalist. Because the fundamental unit of labor at each specialized level can be achieved by AI — and what's left is the ability to coordinate across diverse problem sets.

> "It's definitely the age of the polymath. I believe we're seeing a Renaissance of thinkers — folks who can work across really diverse contexts because they figured out how to leverage AI at the execution layer, and they're now orchestrating across finance, marketing, engineering, growth — all at the same time."

The one-person billion-dollar startup isn't a meme anymore. When AI agents can replicate the output of 20-30 people, a single person with the right systems can build a real business.

### 13. What You Should Do Right Now (5 min)

Concrete, actionable advice:

**1. Build a personal AI budget.**
I spend a fixed amount every month on AI tools. Some are constants (ChatGPT, Claude). Some I rotate — explore, see what's useful at this stage, what fits into my workflow. Come back in a few months and check progress.

**2. Build things that showcase AI fluency in your domain.**

| If you're in... | Build this... |
|-----------------|---------------|
| Finance | A trading bot that identifies opportunities, runs technical analysis, simulates outcomes, scores recommendations |
| Marketing | Brand concept generators, social media agents that identify memetic opportunities and produce content end-to-end |
| Operations | Workflow automation agents, context-gathering systems |
| Any domain | Fake-door websites to market-test ideas, growth experiments using AI |

> "If you're a finance grad interviewing at an investment bank, walk in already showing off a working financial model powered by AI. That's why they're going to hire you — not your degree."

**3. Think in workflows, not tools.**
Don't just "use ChatGPT." Design end-to-end workflows where AI handles each step. Document them. Build context. The more context you build, the more nuanced your AI becomes.

**4. Become a generalist.**
The expectation has fundamentally changed. You will be expected to prototype, design, code, analyze, and ship. The barrier to entry on all of these is now near-zero. The barrier to being *good* at orchestrating them? That's where your value lies.

## ACT VI: THE RELIEF (5 min)

### 14. Closing — Return to the Anxiety Question

> "Let's come back to where we started. I asked how many of you feel anxious about the future because of AI."

Pause. Then:

> "Here's what I want you to take away. That anxiety? It's the same anxiety people felt when the steam engine arrived. When the assembly line was invented. When the internet showed up. Every single time, the world felt like it was ending for the people in it. And every single time, the people who leaned into the change — who learned the new tools, who saw the pattern, who built instead of froze — they didn't just survive. They defined the next era."

> "You are sitting in one of the finest academic institutions in this country. You have access to tools that would have cost millions of dollars a decade ago — for free. You can build a product tonight. You can prototype a business idea by tomorrow morning. You can run a marketing experiment before your next class."

> "The ceiling on intellectual labor that I talked about — it feels like a wall if you're standing under it. But it's actually a launchpad if you're the one building the systems that sit on top of it."

> "We may be moving into an age of abundance. If that's true, it's the most extraordinary opportunity any generation has ever had to create. And if it's not true? Then the people who built the muscle — who learned to work with AI, who designed the workflows, who understood the new economics — they're still the ones who'll be fine."

> "So let me ask again. How many of you feel anxious?"

Pause for the room to react. Likely fewer hands, or a different energy.

> "Good. Now go build something."
