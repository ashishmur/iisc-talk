# Part 2: The AI Revolution Deep Dive

**Source:** Voice memo from Apple Notes "IISC lecture" (memo 1, ~23 min)
**Recorded:** ~March 1, 2026

---

Technology adoption has just changed and there's a lot of value people are already immediately seeing. Now an important challenge of course we've had in this journey is how do you close the loop, right? So we all understand, you have a complex topic, you can go have a discussion on ChatGPT, long conversation, explore ideas, explore things. Get it to summarize, review, give feedback — you're able to move really fast, you're able to produce artifacts. But are you actually able to get a lot of work done? That's the most important question, right? So has it fundamentally increased your productivity, the number of things which you can do — maybe you can replace three other people along with you. Have you reached that sort of a place where time got saved? That's real productivity. And I think for a long time it was very, very difficult because they could never help you close the loop and you still have to have this entire process of human friction over here.

And when you think about AI development, OpenAI's five levels of AGI is one of the most important maps to understand how things would change across the world. So ChatGPT is launched and everything that we saw was almost level one, level two AI where it could respond interestingly. Reasoning started coming in, people said you could start solving problems for them — the beginning of your homework and assignments.

**Level three is agents and agency** — that's a big part of what we do. I drive the development of Excel agent. Agents fundamentally are systems that can pursue a goal repeatedly by reasoning, by planning, by using tools to act inside an environment, and having memory and context because of which they are continuously learning. That's a very interesting paradigm when you think about what an agent is. And when we talk about planning, reasoning, acting — they do it really well.

So for the first time we've started seeing that the entire process where there's a signal which triggers a certain workflow for an information worker — which requires them to understand a certain situation, build cognition, find an answer, take action and close the loop — that process has pretty much completely been automated. Because an agent can interact, engage, understand, go perform and act. And agents exist in all kinds — the environment is not just a digital environment, it's a physical environment. Robots.

In a digital environment, one of the first agents which ever came out was coding agents. They gave access to a computer to be able to write and access files inside it. And one of the first things it could do is read files, edit files and create files — mostly code files. And there's a rich repository of code files available in the world. 100% of all of the work when you talk about signal-to-closure exists for coding. And because of which it's one of the first and best places the models have been trained.

What you end up seeing is when you look at the economy horizontally, the first vertical where there was a lot of focus by labs, especially Anthropic, was how do we crack coding and software development? OpenAI in general has been pursuing a very general strategy. They've had media with Sora, social, education, image generation, enterprise, API, Codex as a coding agent, the consumer product — they're a pretty diverse organization trying to figure out how to get AI to be general and scale. But what you're already seeing is if you can focus resources and solve specific problems, the jump has been massive — especially coding has been one of the best examples.

Today, the paradigm we live in is: single individual engineers can spin up sponsored agents which can read an entire spec doc — the fundamental input document for any engineer — and completely build the product, reach out to you with a video of what they've built, what the flow of the application is, and get confirmation if you're perfectly cool with it. And they can host these applications completely end to end. So you don't have to be somebody who's even literate in terms of what software engineering or coding is. Anybody can start building a product in this market. **The cost to build has come down to zero.**

That is, in a sense, we already have our first experiment/playground to understand what is going to happen to careers when you start scaling AI. First things: it's an even playing field for everyone. There's no expert domain. There's no domain expertise for which you have to spend a lot of money to acquire and pay a lot of money in the form of labor to consume. So the cost of the product of what you need has fundamentally collapsed. There's deflation inside the economy. The cost to build software has come down to zero.

It's also a fascinating time for product managers — we'll come to that part.

With the cost of production coming down to zero, a lot of senior software engineers have completely stopped coding by themselves. They're mostly leading 20 agents who complete bulk of the work by themselves. "Vibe coding" has become a key term now — by Andrej Karpathy, the head of AI for Tesla, worked at OpenAI, worked with Google DeepMind. He's completely embraced it.

His viral tweet really pretty much summarizes what's happened even to the industry of product management. Second tweet by Neville Ravikant, where he says product management is vibe coding and engineering has become prompt engineering. So the ideas people — who fundamentally had to work with managing growth over risk, and the tool you fundamentally use is technology — you have engineers who can build a product out for you and the biggest risk fundamentally is the cost of engineering itself. With cost of production going down to zero: one, you could try some of your riskiest bets if you can figure out compute. Two, you could try out a lot of risky stuff in parallel. You can have five different prototypes of the product experience and go test that out inside your usability study.

You also realize that one function has started subsuming what another function does. Now of course there's a lot of progress which has to be made in terms of how AI agents can work on scalable machine infrastructure. The fact that they have already replaced a complete layer or two layers of a workforce in an industry is a big deal. We are already seeing the first steps of what that's resulting in.

The outcome is pretty certain and clear — sweeping layoffs across organizations. Block announced 50% of their employees are being laid off specifically because of AI. Stock markets taking a beating for IT companies — we've seen 25 to 30% drops in their shares just because of the progress. Devin, Cognition's coding agent. But despite the fact that the cost of production of software has come down and is going to become pretty much zero, it isn't a complete deflation where there's infinite amount of software available for everybody. People still pay as consumers and as a producer, you can't potentially get it to everybody.

**So distribution and marketing have become a very important problem.** A very close friend of mine runs his own technology consulting IT firm — tells me that the new job for everyone has become marketing. That's what he has been able to do and that's what he's getting his team to go to. Distribution has become the big challenge to fight over. We will definitely see those sorts of repositioning of effort and fundamental blockers in terms of completely unlocking productivity end to end.

Which definitely does bring us to this question: would marketing jobs be at risk? If you do look at the progress in terms of AI adoption — yes, it's not been as high as it is in legal or in marketing, but they are next in line and progress is happening there too.

Now the fundamental question is: because we don't have complete data in terms of this signal-to-closure loop, a lot of these jobs have other domains — this physical domain, interactions between people which are offline. How would an AI really understand how to perform all of the tasks over there with the nuance and uniqueness and the right intelligence? Is it really a general intelligence just because we have replaced software engineers? We really don't know. The AI labs believe that reaching a certain level of intelligence even at the software engineering stage will slowly allow them to figure out how to diffuse that into a more horizontal layer — and the fact that the data doesn't exist is not going to be a large block. Truth is, you really don't know. This could be one of the primary reasons we just don't make it through.

Economically speaking, there are a lot more implications not just in terms of business, but also the whole of society. There's a pretty popular financial research paper — one of the strong philosophies where there's two sides to the entire economic argument now in terms of how AI would play out.

**Side 1 — Deflation/Abundance:** There might be hundreds of processes and hundreds of people in a loop which get a service to you. Each of them costs something in terms of labor. Each of them adds time, each of them adds inefficiencies, each of them adds failures. That's a cost and a burden we put on society. And the belief is that if we remove them and reposition them somewhere else in society, there should be a fundamental deflation playing out. So things also start becoming cheaper. And yes, you are not making as much money — but a lot of its effects would be subdued or numbed down because of a deflationary pressure on the cost of goods throughout the ecosystem. The belief that we will enter the era of abundance.

Not that our economics have ever taught us to be prepared for such a scenario. It doesn't exist. You've broken the entire economic equation fundamentally. Because we're introducing a zero or near-zero factor. It's only really the cost of compute. And the optimist also believes that there will be more jobs which go around — the same theory we just discussed.

We should introduce this somewhere in the story — this whole deflation versus what else is going to happen.

**Where's the value in the AI industry and ecosystem?** What's the strategy? How do you survive in this new world? There's multiple levels: energy providers, chip manufacturers, fabricators, frontier model labs, hyperscalers, the application layer, the intermediaries, the platforms. Everyone else which comes beyond that. So where's the value really accruing to?

A couple of other things. The minute we talk about agents, another interesting thing is **agents don't need UI** because UI was meant for humans. Agents can communicate with each other without UI — which is **Model Context Protocol (MCP)**, something which Anthropic introduced over a year ago. Which means the application layer is actually under threat. There is no need to have 10 different products, apps, 100 different websites that you open up. You would just talk to your AI, and your AI would get everything done. Why would you subject yourself to friction which is UI?

But you don't need to have a platform built out on top of this. Nobody really knows if you could forego the app layer. You have tons of products. Microsoft continues to build the Excel agent. We still are completely focused on the UI layer. ChatGPT/OpenAI in fact has a ChatGPT marketplace — they believe one of the long-term business opportunities is turning into a platform on top of which app stores can drive applications towards consumers, driven by the models which they have built. And in turn they tax the companies 30% just like what Apple does.

Another thesis: **the model is a commodity.** Anthropic got into a fight with the department of defense in the U.S. saying they weren't willing to sign a contract where the U.S. government could do anything with the models. The U.S. government in turn just said, "Cool, thank you very much. We're bringing in the next model provider." ChatGPT went ahead and actually signed a contract with the U.S. government. And the government has just said, "Everyone, it doesn't matter. You can just swap the model out."

It's the context which you have, the workflows that you build, the processes which you use, the agents which you make interact in terms of how they get work done. Who needs the model? You can switch any model. It's a commodity. In fact, that's the current strategy at Microsoft. We allow you to pick any model in any of our products. You can use every single model. I have access to every single model as an employee at Microsoft. We have no set preferences. Everything is interchangeable. We don't think the value lies there. We think the value lies in the compute layer — all of the organizational knowledge, processes, systems which you have put in to get work done.

The first way you do it is you don't break the workflows and processes — integrate AI into each of those steps, replicating exactly the way the human processes that piece of information work. But over time, when it's just agents talking to each other, you have opportunities to re-architect the idea of how an organization is actually structured. This actually puts to threat a lot of fundamental organizational behavior and human resources concepts.

**Organizations might become a lot smaller.** There is a lot of talk and excitement for the first single-person billion-dollar startup. They also say it's one of the best times to go out and build a product by yourself, build a business by yourself, because you have leverage of AI agents which can replicate the actions of 20 to 30 different human beings if you really optimize them well. Of course they work in very specific domains — they have not had the same horizontal diffusion across all industries and job profiles.

That potentially is what gets us to **stage four**, where we start speaking about AI organizations. If you are somebody who is following the product space, this is where you start thinking about where things are. This is where they're going to get to. We are already at agents. This is where we're going to start getting to organizations in the AI space. We're going to look at agents start seeing agents come out in other industries.

A single person startup especially in the information technology space is quite exciting. In that context, I think **it's one of the best times to be a product manager.** What's the core metric of PM? Managing growth over risk in a way where you're able to deliver significant value. I feel leaders with whom I've worked believe that if 50% of your projects don't fail as a PM, you're not a good PM. That's pretty true.

And across organizations, the one thing I've noticed is that your fastest opportunity to grow is your **ability to think long term.** Thinking long term in a VUCA world is very difficult. There is uncertainty. Uncertainty is just growing because technology is changing around you rapidly. It's changing the way people do things. You need to constantly plan for where the puck will be, but the puck is not even really set right now.

You will be expected to fail, but that's okay because the kind of skills you build along the way — the kind of expertise which it is going to expect you to bring in is somebody who's an innovator, a visionary. And you're also able to figure out how you take this product to customers — your GTM.

If it's the best time to build a single business or a startup, then it's the best time to be a PM because it just means that you have an opportunity to go build a digital business, monetize a digital opportunity without the fundamental friction you've always had — which is sprint planning, resource prioritization. The most dreadful part of a product manager's job is the fact that somebody only gives you 12 engineers and you need to figure out everything you want to do within that. That's your budget. And now the cost for all that has come down to zero.

**The innovator's dilemma.** I've been amazed and obsessed about the fact that every company in the tech space is going through the innovator's dilemma, but their response has been pretty unified. Everyone is clearly seeing the death of their economic and business model on the wall — to the point where they have decided to abandon their entire focus in terms of doing anything in their existing business and identify what space they can build out for themselves in the ecosystem. Companies that don't have a strategy have always been thrown off by the markets. We should talk about the frequency AI has been mentioned in financial disclosures.
